Hiring Algorithm

A narrative game where you step into the role of an automated HR system. The bias isn't explained — it's produced by your own decisions.

Challenge

Making invisible bias tangible

Algorithmic bias is abstract. You can read about it, watch talks about it, agree that it's a problem — and still not feel how it works. The challenge: create an experience where bias isn't a topic, but something the player actively produces.

Approach

Procedural rhetoric as method

Bogost's concept of procedural rhetoric says that a game's rules carry the argument — not its text, not its cutscenes. The Hiring Algorithm uses this principle: the player evaluates candidates through mechanics that encode real biases from Bolukbasi et al.'s research on word embeddings.

"Man is to computer programmer as woman is to homemaker." That's not an opinion — it's a geometric relationship in the data. The game turns that geometry into decisions you make with your own hands.

The prototype is a theory about how algorithmic bias works. Contestable, defensible, substantive.

Following Galey & Ruecker's framework, the prototype itself is a form of argumentation. Each version captures a specific claim about how bias operates, open for criticism and iteration.

Built With

Twine + GitHub Pages

Twine handles the branching narrative structure. The game exports as a single HTML file, hosted directly on GitHub Pages. No backend, no dependencies — the whole argument lives in one self-contained page.

Built for the Gaming Seminar SS2026 at the University of Vienna (Instructor: Arndt Niebisch). The course explores gaming mechanisms in art and humanities, with a focus on user-centric design and prototyping.

Status

In development. First Twine sketches in progress. User testing and iterative documentation planned for the semester. Final paper + game due 15 September 2026.
